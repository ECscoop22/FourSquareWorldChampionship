The rules below represent the official standards used at the Four Square World Championships, held annually in Bridgton, Maine. While Four Square is played around the world with many different rule variations, this rulebook specifically governs the gameplay, court layout, and tournament structure for the World Championship.

For those interested in alternate styles or playground adaptations, we invite you to explore the "Other Rules" section at the bottom of this page.

Court Rules

  • Game is played on a square court divided into four equal quadrants.
  • Each quadrant is assigned a rank: 1 (King), 2, 3, and 4 (lowest).
  • Boundary lines are considered in.

Serving Rules

  • Game begins with the player in square 1 serving to square 2.
  • Serve must be underhand and hit once into the server’s own square before crossing into the receiver’s square.
  • Receiver must allow the ball to bounce once before returning it.

Ball Striking Rules

  • Only hands and wrists may strike the ball. “Hands” are defined as the area from the wrist to the fingertips, including the backs of the hands.
  • The ball may be struck with one or both hands, using either open palms or closed fists, as long as it is one deliberate and clean hit.
  • No carrying, catching, holding, or double-hits (hitting the ball twice in a row) are allowed at any time.
  • Unnatural spins are permitted only if the spin is the result of a single legal strike—not a carry or scoop.
  • Any player who strikes the ball illegally is considered out for that round.

Faults & Eliminations

  • If the ball bounces twice in a player's square, they are out.
  • If a player hits the ball out of bounds, they are out.
  • Interference with another player’s space is grounds for elimination.

Advancement

  • When a player is eliminated, the remaining players advance to fill the vacated squares in order of rank.
  • A new player joins in square 4.

Other Rules

  • Variations may exist in casual or playground settings, such as using feet, playing with more than four squares, or modifying serve rules.
  • These variations are not part of the official World Championship rules but may be enjoyed recreationally.
